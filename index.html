Ian Padilla-Gay

San Diego, CA, United States

I am an N3AS Postdoctoral Fellow at the University of California, Berkeley, and a Visiting Scholar at Fuller Lab, UC San Diego. I earned my PhD in Physics from the University of Copenhagen in October 2022.

I aim to uncover the role of neutrinos–fundamental particles–in supernova explosions.

My research focuses on neutrino flavor conversion and heavy-element nucleosynthesis in dense astrophysical environments. I am interested in approximating the effects of collective neutrino oscillations in state-of-the-art hydrodynamical simulations and studying the conditions and outcomes of heavy-element nucleosynthesis. At SLAC/Stanford, I investigated how elements heavier than iron—such as Molybdenum-92,94, Ruthenium-96,98, and Niobium-92—are produced, and how general relativistic effects, supernova dynamics, and neutrino heating influence this process. Currently, at UC San Diego, I am investigating the occurnce of flavor instabilities in dense neutrino gases, as well as the role of neutrino oscillations in producing important pronton-rich isotopes in the universe.

  1. JCAP
    vp-process in core-collapse supernovae: imprints of general relativistic effects
    Alexander Friedland, Derek J. Li, Giuseppe Lucente, Ian Padilla-Gay, and 1 more author
    JCAP, 2026
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    The origin of a number of proton-rich isotopes in the solar system has been a long-standing puzzle. A promising explanation is the νp-process, which is thought to occur in neutrino-driven outflows that form inside core-collapse supernovae after shock revival. While recent studies have analyzed several physical effects that influence the efficiency of this process, the impact of General Relativity (GR) has not yet been explored. Here we perform a comparative analysis of the time-integrated νp-process yields in Newtonian and fully GR calculations, using detailed models of time-evolving outflow profiles. GR effects are found to suppress the production of seed nuclei, which significantly boosts the resulting p-nuclide abundances. Our reference GR model, with an 18 M⊙ progenitor, reproduces both the relative and absolute solar-system abundances of the full set of p-nuclides in the mass range 74 ≤ A ≤ 102. The yields are less optimal in our 12.75 M⊙ GR model, where the outflow transitions to the supersonic regime several seconds into the explosion, suppressing further p-nuclide production. In both models, most of the production of the key p-isotopes 92,94Mo and 96,98Ru occurs relatively early, about 1–3 seconds after shock revival. In contrast, a large fraction of the shielded isotope 92Nb is produced in the later ejecta. The impact of GR on this isotope is particularly strong, with its final abundance increased by a factor of 25 compared to a Newtonian calculation. In summary, when GR effects are included, the νp-process in a sufficiently massive progenitor can provide a unified explanation for the origin of all p-nuclei in the solar system up to 102Pd.

  2. PRD
    Flavor equilibration of supernova neutrinos: Exploring the dynamics of slow modes
    Ian Padilla-Gay, Heng-Hao Chen, Sajad Abbar, Meng-Ru Wu, and 1 more author
    Phys. Rev. D, 2025
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    Neutrinos experience collective flavor conversion in extreme astrophysical environments such as core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe). One manifestation of collective conversion is slow flavor conversion (SFC), which has recently attracted renewed interest because of its ubiquity across different regions of the supernova environment. In this study, we systematically examine the evolution of kinematic decoherence in a dense neutrino gas undergoing SFC, considering lepton number asymmetries as large as 30%. Our findings show that the neutrino gas asymptotically evolves toward a generic state of coarse-grained flavor equilibration that is constrained by approximate lepton number conservation. The equilibration occurs within a few factors of the inverse vacuum oscillation frequency, ω⁻¹, corresponding to (anti)neutrinos reaching near flavor equipartition after traveling only a few kilometers for typical supernova neutrino energies. Notably, the quasi-steady state of the neutrino number densities can be quantitatively described by the neutrino–antineutrino number density ratio n_\barν_e / n_ν_e alone. Such a simple estimate opens new opportunities for incorporating SFC into CCSN simulations, particularly in regions where SFC develops on scales much shorter than those of collisions.

  3. JCAP
    Symmetry breaking due to multi-angle matter-neutrino resonance in neutron star merger remnants
    Ian Padilla-Gay, Shashank Shalgar, and Irene Tamborra
    JCAP, 2024
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    Neutron star merger remnants are unique sites for exploring neutrino flavor conversion in dense media. Because of the natural excess of antielectron neutrinos over electron neutrinos, the neutrino–neutrino potential can cancel the matter potential, giving rise to matter–neutrino resonant flavor conversion. Under the assumption of two (anti)neutrino flavors and spatial homogeneity, we solve the neutrino quantum kinetic equations to investigate the occurrence of the matter–neutrino resonance within a multi-angle framework. We find that isotropy is broken spontaneously, regardless of the mass ordering. Using a hydrodynamical simulation of a binary neutron star merger remnant with a black hole of 3 solar masses and an accretion torus of 0.3 solar masses, we find that complete flavor conversion caused by the matter–neutrino resonance is unlikely, even though the matter and neutrino potentials cancel at various locations above the disk. Importantly, matter–neutrino resonant flavor conversion depends crucially on the shape of the neutrino angular distributions. Our findings suggest that accurate modeling of the neutrino angular distributions is necessary to understand flavor conversion physics in merger remnants, as well as its implications for disk physics and the synthesis of elements heavier than iron.

  4. PRD
    Collisions and collective flavor conversion: Integrating out the fast dynamics
    Damiano F. G. Fiorillo, Ian Padilla-Gay, and Georg G. Raffelt
    Phys. Rev. D, 2024
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    In dense astrophysical environments, notably core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers, neutrino–neutrino forward scattering can trigger flavor conversion on very short scales. Scattering with the background medium can influence collective flavor conversion in several ways, either damping oscillations or potentially triggering new collisional flavor instabilities (CFIs). A key aspect of this process is the relative slowness of collisions compared to the much faster dynamics of neutrino–neutrino refraction. Assuming spatial homogeneity, we exploit this hierarchy of scales to simplify the description by accounting only for the slow dynamics driven by collisions. We illustrate our approach both for CFIs and for fast flavor instabilities that are damped by collisions. In both cases, our method leads to a new set of equations, the slow-dynamics equations, which simplify the description of flavor conversion and allow a qualitative understanding of the final state of the system once the instability—either collisional or fast—has saturated.

  5. PRD
    Neutrino fast flavor pendulum. II. Collisional damping
    Ian Padilla-Gay, Irene Tamborra, and Georg G. Raffelt
    Phys. Rev. D, 2022
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    In compact astrophysical objects, the neutrino density can be so high that neutrino–neutrino refraction can lead to fast flavor conversion of the type ν̄e νe ↔ ν̄x νx with x = μ, τ, depending on the neutrino angular distribution. Previously, we showed that in a homogeneous, axisymmetric two-flavor system, these collective solutions evolve in analogy with a gyroscopic pendulum. In flavor space, the deviation from the weak-interaction direction is quantified by a variable cos θ that moves between +1 and cos θ_min, where the latter follows from a linear mode analysis. As a next step, we include collisional damping of flavor coherence, assuming a common damping rate Γ for all modes. Empirically, we find that the damped pendular motion reaches an asymptotic level of pair conversion f = A + (1 − A) cos θ_min with A ≈ 0.370. This result does not depend on details of the angular distribution (except through cos θ_min), the initial seed, or Γ. On the other hand, even a small asymmetry between the neutrino and antineutrino damping rates significantly alters this picture and can even enable flavor instabilities in systems that would otherwise remain stable.

  6. PRL
    Neutrino Flavor Pendulum Reloaded: The Case of Fast Pairwise Conversion
    Ian Padilla-Gay, Irene Tamborra, and Georg G. Raffelt
    Phys. Rev. Lett., 2022
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    In core-collapse supernovae or compact binary merger remnants, neutrino–neutrino refraction can trigger fast pair conversion of the type ν̄e νe ↔ ν̄x νx (with x = μ, τ), governed by the angle-dependent density matrices of flavor lepton number. In a homogeneous and axially symmetric two-flavor system, all angular modes evolve coherently. We show that the nonlinear equations of motion are formally equivalent to those of a gyroscopic pendulum. Within this analogy, our main innovation is identifying the key property of the lepton-number angular distribution that determines the depth of conversion with the “pendulum spin.” This quantity is given by the real part of the eigenfrequency obtained from the linear normal-mode analysis of the neutrino system. This simple analogy allows one to predict the depth of flavor conversion without solving the full nonlinear evolution equations. Our approach therefore provides a novel diagnostic tool for exploring the physics of nonlinear systems.

  7. JHEP
    A three Higgs doublet model with symmetry-suppressed flavour changing neutral currents
    Dipankar Das, P. M. Ferreira, António P. Morais, Ian Padilla-Gay, and 2 more authors
    JHEP, 2021
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    We construct a three-Higgs-doublet model with a flavor non-universal U(1) × Z₂ symmetry. This symmetry induces suppressed flavor-changing interactions mediated by neutral scalars. New scalars with masses below the TeV scale can still satisfy the constraints arising from flavor data. Such a model can therefore motivate direct searches for additional Higgs bosons in future collider experiments and includes a nontrivial flavor structure.

  8. JCAP
    Multi-Dimensional Solution of Fast Neutrino Conversions in Binary Neutron Star Merger Remnants
    Ian Padilla-Gay, Shashank Shalgar, and Irene Tamborra
    JCAP, 2021
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    Fast pairwise conversions of neutrinos are predicted to be ubiquitous in neutron star merger remnants, with potentially major implications for the nucleosynthesis of elements heavier than iron. We present the first sophisticated numerical solution of the neutrino flavor evolution above the remnant disk within a (2+1+1)-dimensional setup: two spatial coordinates, one angular variable, and time. We search for a steady-state flavor configuration above the remnant disk. Although linear stability analysis predicts flavor instabilities at all locations above the remnant disk, our nonlinear simulations show that fast pairwise conversions lead to minimal neutrino mixing (less than 1%); flavor equilibration is never achieved in our models. Importantly, fast neutrino conversions are more prominent in localized regions near the edges of the (anti)neutrino decoupling surfaces and are almost negligible in the polar region of the remnant. Our findings on the role of fast pairwise conversions should be interpreted with caution because of the approximations inherent in our setup, and they motivate further studies within a more realistic framework.

  9. JCAP
    Neutrino propagation hinders fast pairwise flavor conversions
    Shashank Shalgar, Ian Padilla-Gay, and Irene Tamborra
    JCAP, 2020
    Abs DOI arXiv Bib

    Neutrino flavor conversions may dramatically affect the inner workings of compact astrophysical objects as well as the synthesis of heavy elements. We present the first sophisticated numerical solution of neutrino flavor conversion within a (2+1+1)-dimensional setup: we include the advective term in the neutrino equations of motion and track the flavor evolution in two spatial dimensions, one angular variable, and time. Notably, the advective term hinders the development of neutrino pairwise conversions if the conditions favoring such conversions—such as crossings between the angular distributions of νe and ν̄e, or a non-negligible flux of neutrinos traveling backward with respect to the main propagation direction—exist for timescales shorter than the typical timescale associated with advection. As a consequence, fast pairwise conversions can occur only when the conditions favoring flavor conversion are self-sustained and global, such as those induced by the lepton-emission self-sustained asymmetry (LESA) in core-collapse supernovae. Our work highlights the major impact of the dynamical evolution of the neutrino field on the growth of flavor instabilities and the strong interplay between classical and quantum effects. It also points out critical limitations of linear stability analysis, which is commonly used to predict neutrino flavor instabilities.
